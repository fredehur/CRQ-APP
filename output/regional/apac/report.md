## Why — Geopolitical Driver

Seerist detected a geospatial anomaly in the Taipei industrial district on 19 March 2026, with a deviation score of 0.87 — the highest anomaly-flagged location in APAC during the current collection window. The anomaly coincided with the Kaohsiung dockworker strike entering its second day and with elevated South China Sea patrol assertiveness near the Spratly Islands, placing Taiwan's manufacturing corridor under simultaneous labour disruption and cross-strait pressure. Seerist rates the regional risk trajectory as declining, with an average pulse delta of -0.6 across China and Taiwan, and its country-level assessment rates China as High on both political and security dimensions. Seerist Scribe characterises China's state-directed industrial policy as explicitly prioritising domestic energy technology at the expense of foreign operators, and assesses that People's Liberation Army-linked cyber operations targeting Taiwan-based critical infrastructure represent an elevated and enduring risk. OSINT corroborates this structural picture: Norway formally warned of security risks from Chinese technology dependencies in wind and solar supply chains — a government-level acknowledgment that Chinese component dominance creates strategic exposure for foreign renewable energy operators, as reported by Recharge. The China-US green technology trade dispute escalated in 2026, with China's Ministry of Commerce opening a formal probe into US green product trade barriers, reported by Digitimes. China's dominance of global offshore wind capacity, confirmed by Outlook Business, amplifies the structural incentive for state-directed acquisition of Western aerodynamic design advantages through non-market means. These are not transient signals — they describe a reinforcing structural condition in which the strategic premium on Western wind turbine IP is rising while legitimate technology transfer pathways are narrowing.

## How — Cyber Vector

Chinese state-sponsored actors have been confirmed by Check Point, as reported by Infosecurity Magazine, to be targeting manufacturing firms specifically for intellectual property theft. The campaign is sector-confirmed against manufacturing without a publicly named threat actor — indicating deliberate operational security. A documented intrusion lifecycle from a Norwegian wind and energy sector incident, also reported by Infosecurity Magazine, shows what execution of this threat vector looks like in practice: the attacker maintained undetected access from March to July 2025 through internal reconnaissance and credential theft before deploying destructive code in December 2025 — a nine-month presence consistent with intellectual property collection rather than immediate disruption. EU sanctions proceedings confirm that Chinese and Iranian threat actors have been executing credential theft campaigns — including automated credential harvesting techniques — against energy and manufacturing targets, corroborated by BleepingComputer. On the Iranian dimension, CISA guidance and Horizon3.ai reporting assess that Iranian APT groups are actively targeting energy sector OT and manufacturing networks; confirmed incidents are concentrated in Middle East and US contexts — APAC-specific Iranian activity has not been confirmed in current signals. Seerist's own WOD search on ransomware targeting energy OT networks returned results confirming the sector is under sustained targeting pressure, consistent with system intrusion as the highest-frequency incident scenario globally. No named threat actor or confirmed incident in the current signal corpus is directly attributed to any APAC wind turbine manufacturing facility or OT environment — the threat assessment is grounded in sector-level pattern and the Norwegian precedent, not in confirmed intrusion against any APAC-based operator.

## So What — Business Impact

If a system intrusion achieves persistent access to AeroGrid's Kaohsiung Manufacturing Hub, the Series 7 production line — representing 60% of global turbine output — and SCADA network TW-01 could be compromised before any indication of attacker presence is detected, consistent with the nine-month undetected dwell documented in the Norwegian incident. The Seerist hotspot anomaly in the Taipei industrial district places AeroGrid's primary APAC manufacturing site within an active disruption zone. Supply chain dependencies on Taiwanese semiconductor components and Korean rare earth supply extend the attack surface beyond the manufacturing floor. The TEPCO five-year turbine supply agreement and the Vestas APAC logistics joint venture mean that production delays or IP loss translate directly into contractual delivery risk and partner confidence exposure. VaCR for this exposure is $18,500,000, mapped to the system intrusion scenario — financial rank 3 in the CRQ register, frequency rank 1 globally. Current signals are trend-level: structural conditions strongly favour targeting, but no confirmed attempt against AeroGrid's APAC footprint has been identified. Three developments would indicate the threat is materialising: first, a publicly reported intrusion at a peer wind energy manufacturer involving OT dwell time or access to aerodynamic design files at a Taiwan or Japan facility; second, new military exercise activity in the Taiwan Strait or a Seerist anomaly escalation in the Taipei industrial corridor beyond the current 0.87 deviation score; third, additional government-level warnings or formal sanctions naming Chinese-supplied wind energy components as a confirmed attack vector — following Norway's supply chain warning — which would indicate the intelligence community has confirmed a broader active campaign.
