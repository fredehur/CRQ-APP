## Why — Geopolitical Driver

A confirmed military escalation in the Gulf has moved from background risk to active supply chain event. Reporting by The Guardian on 25 March 2026 confirms that Iran-linked missile and projectile strikes on merchant vessels in the Gulf of Oman and Strait of Hormuz approaches — corroborated by a JMIC maritime threat assessment elevated to CRITICAL, as documented by Hellenic Shipping News — have effectively halted commercial transit through the corridor. The consequence for European offshore wind is already operational: the same Guardian reporting documents confirmed delivery delays to large EU and UK offshore wind projects as turbine and blade component shipments are stranded by the disruption. Iran's control over four maritime chokepoints — reported by MSN and confirmed in a Global Banking and Finance Review account of direct IRGC warnings to shipping — removes the possibility of a workable bypass; simultaneous constraint of both the Strait of Hormuz and the Bab al-Mandab eliminates alternative routing for Gulf-origin components. The EU Commission's 2026 Energy Security Package, assessed by the EU Institute for Security Studies in its briefs on war footing and critical infrastructure, has formally designated offshore wind as critical infrastructure under the NIS2 and CER Directives — meaning this supply chain disruption is occurring precisely as European wind operators are being elevated into the regulated critical infrastructure tier, raising their strategic value as targets. Open Access Government's coverage of Europe's energy independence push frames this as the EU's decisive inflection year for renewable deployment, a strategic priority that adversarial states have clear incentive to frustrate.

## How — Cyber Vector

Nation-state actors have demonstrated confirmed targeting of European energy OT environments, establishing the adversarial tradecraft relevant to operators in this theatre. Geopolitical Matters, drawing on CISA and Five Eyes assessments, confirms that Volt Typhoon — attributed to Chinese state sponsorship — has pre-positioned within European critical infrastructure OT networks in an embedded access posture consistent with long-dwell intelligence collection rather than immediate disruption; this tradecraft is directly applicable to wind SCADA environments holding predictive maintenance algorithms and live farm telemetry. The same source documents Sandworm's confirmed 2023 attack against 22 Danish energy companies through exploitation of industrial control systems, producing operational disruption across a peer European wind sector cohort. EU Institute for Security Studies and Chatham House reporting confirms the broader assessment that European energy infrastructure has become a primary vector for adversarial hybrid operations combining physical and cyber pressure. A structural enabling condition is documented by IEU Monitoring and Wavestone: 26 EU member states are subject to infringement decisions for failing to transpose NIS2 and CER Directives, creating systemic OT security blind spots across wind operators that adversarial actors are assessed to be mapping as exploitable attack surfaces. TopSeven's reporting on wind turbine inspection data sovereignty highlights an additional exposure: European operators increasingly rely on externally processed inspection and telemetry data, creating third-party access vectors.
