## Why — Geopolitical Driver

Iran has moved from rhetorical threat to legislative instrument: in March 2026, Tehran enacted measures imposing transit fees on oil and gas carriers transiting the Strait of Hormuz, formalising a durable leverage mechanism over one of the world's most consequential energy shipping chokepoints. The direct consequence for the renewable energy sector is already visible — corroborated by The Guardian in reporting from 25 March 2026, Hormuz closure fears have delayed large offshore wind component shipments destined for EU and UK projects, exposing a structural dependency on maritime routes for the European wind infrastructure buildout. European governments have responded by leading a coordinated diplomatic push to reopen the Strait — evidenced by NEPM/NPR — confirming that the disruption has crossed from geopolitical signal into operational consequence for energy supply chains. CSIS and the ODNI have assessed Iran's demonstrated intent and capability to use energy infrastructure targeting as an instrument of geopolitical coercion, establishing this as a deliberate, sustained pressure strategy. The Mediterranean corridor and North African overland logistics routes — both feeders for Southern European wind energy manufacturing — sit directly inside this contested maritime theatre. Analysis by TenBizT Military Analysis notes Iran's Strait control is generating cascading effects across European industrial supply chains with no near-term diplomatic resolution in sight.

## How — Cyber Vector

The geopolitical coercion observed at the maritime layer has a confirmed cyber counterpart. CISA, the FBI, NSA, and DC3 issued a joint advisory in June 2025 confirming that MuddyWater — aligned to Iran's Ministry of Intelligence and Security — has pre-positioned persistent access via Dindoor and Fakeset backdoors inside critical infrastructure supply chain firms, airports, and financial institutions. The tradecraft is patient: pre-positioning rather than immediate disruption, consistent with intelligence collection and leverage-building ahead of potential activation. Concurrently, CyberAv3ngers, operating under the IRGC Cyber Electronic Command, has deployed IOCONTROL malware against internet-exposed industrial control and OT devices at scale; Censys data cited by CISA identifies 40,000–48,000 such exposed devices as an active attack surface, indicating the group has developed targeting capability at industrial scale. The ODNI, Industrial Cyber reporting, and Inside Cybersecurity all confirm the broader assessment that Iran represents an escalating cyber risk to critical infrastructure globally. Assessed: Iran's documented OT pre-positioning in analogous critical infrastructure sectors — combined with the confirmed Strait leverage strategy — creates a plausible intrusion pathway for energy operators with Mediterranean supply chain exposure. Collection gap acknowledged: no confirmed incident of wind-sector-specific IP exfiltration or system intrusion in the MED corridor has been identified in the current collection window; the threat assessment derives from confirmed capability, documented intent, and transferable sector tradecraft, not from a named MED incident.

## So What — Business Impact

The disruption risk for AeroGrid's Mediterranean footprint is operational before it is financial. Palermo (manufacturing, HIGH criticality) depends on the Mediterranean supply corridor and the North African logistics route — both within the Hormuz-disrupted maritime theatre — for component delivery into the offshore blade assembly process. Delays to EU offshore wind component shipments already confirmed by The Guardian translate directly into production schedule risk at Palermo. The Malaga service hub carries exposure through the Iberdrola offshore maintenance contract (4-year) and the ENEL MED service agreement — both dependent on Malaga's ability to mobilise components and field service capacity without supply chain interruption. On the intrusion side, the system intrusion scenario — ranked #3 globally by financial impact in the CRQ master register, accounting for 21.4% of global financial impact — is the scenario consistent with Iran's confirmed OT pre-positioning tradecraft. The MED VaCR is $4,200,000, assessed against the Predictive Maintenance and Offshore IP Theft scenario; the offshore blade assembly process and Mediterranean service network are the primary crown jewels at risk. Watch for the following as materialisation signals: Iran escalating from transit fee legislation to active vessel interdiction or enforced blockade actions in the Hormuz strait; a confirmed intrusion or pre-positioning event at a European offshore wind component manufacturer or logistics provider serving the Mediterranean basin; or disruption to the North African overland corridor serving Palermo's inbound component flows.
