**Intelligence Assessment:** B/2 — Corroborated indicators, probably true. | Signal type: Trend

## Why — Geopolitical Driver

Two intersecting forces are reshaping the strategic environment for wind energy supply chains across Latin America. China's state-sponsored industrial espionage apparatus — documented by Marketplace as a systematic instrument of national industrial policy — has consistently targeted renewable energy technology manufacturers, with particular focus on wind turbine design software and power electronics. This is not an inferred risk: Chinese state-linked enterprises have been prosecuted in Western courts for stealing wind turbine control software from competitors, establishing both the intent and the operational method. In parallel, Russia has used strategic supply agreements and large-scale infrastructure projects across LATAM to cultivate political and economic dependencies, corroborated by the Center for the Study of Democracy as a deliberate influence tool in the region. Neither actor pursues these strategies in isolation — both stand to benefit commercially and strategically from degrading Western renewable energy technology advantages in markets where they are expanding their own presence. The third structural driver: LATAM holds approximately 40% of global copper production, a critical input for wind turbine components. The U.S.-China rivalry over access to this resource base is intensifying, as documented by CSIS, JP Morgan Private Bank, and AIB Insights — with Chinese multinationals facing Western market exclusion and redirecting investment pressure toward Latin American sourcing networks. The combined effect is a region where the material inputs to wind energy infrastructure are contested at the state level by actors who have demonstrated both the intent and the capability to exploit commercial access for strategic advantage.

## How — Cyber Vector

The confirmed precedent establishing actor capability in this sector is the Sinovel Wind Group case: a Chinese state-linked turbine manufacturer convicted in U.S. federal court — corroborated by Reuters, the U.S. Department of Justice, NPR, and Dark Reading — for stealing AMSC wind turbine control software source code through a corrupted insider. The method was supply chain access rather than external network intrusion: a trusted vendor relationship was exploited to extract source code governing turbine operations across multiple markets. This case is analytically material because it confirms that Chinese state-linked actors have both the intent to target wind turbine IP and a demonstrated willingness to use vendor and supply chain access as the primary vector.

Beyond this precedent, cyber collection for LATAM in the current window produced no grounded indicators. No named threat actor targeting LATAM wind energy operations, component supplier networks, or field service management systems has been identified. No confirmed intrusion incident with a named target or attributed method was recovered involving turbine component suppliers or wind farm service infrastructure in the region. The assessment of a current, active cyber campaign directed at LATAM wind operations cannot be supported by available evidence — the cyber pillar rests on geopolitical conditions and sector precedent, not on confirmed regional indicators. This is the primary collection gap: LATAM-specific operational technology intrusion intelligence and current supply chain attribution data remain absent. The B/2 Admiralty rating reflects this: corroborated geopolitical indicators, with cyber confidence held back from B/1 due to the thin collection record.

## So What — Business Impact

AeroGrid's LATAM exposure centres on service delivery continuity for its two active contracts — Enel Chile wind maintenance and Neoenergia turbine service in Brazil — and the 680 staff across the São Paulo Regional Office and Santiago Service Centre who execute them. Both contracts depend on supply chain continuity for turbine components and replacement parts sourced through networks now subject to the structural pressures documented above. AeroGrid holds no manufacturing presence in LATAM; the region's service-only model makes it a direct consumer of component flows rather than a producer with inventory buffers — meaning any degradation of supply chain access translates directly into field service delivery risk. The applicable CRQ scenario is System intrusion (financial rank 3 globally by impact), interpreted here as the pathway through which a state-linked actor, having demonstrated capability via the Sinovel precedent, could reach wind turbine IP or operational systems through supply chain vendor relationships. VaCR is $0 — the CRQ register does not assign a quantified cyber exposure to LATAM operations, reflecting the absence of primary manufacturing assets. Should a confirmed LATAM-specific incident emerge, CRQ inputs would need to be revisited.

Watch for: Chinese state-linked enterprise project awards advancing in Chile's or Brazil's renewable energy sectors in ways that position PRC-affiliated vendors into component supply chains serving the Enel Chile or Neoenergia service agreements — this would signal structural access risk becoming operationally proximate. Watch for U.S. trade actions restricting or tariffing wind turbine components with PRC supply chain exposure, which would convert the current structural pressure into a confirmed sourcing cost event affecting both active contracts. Watch for attribution of a supply chain compromise or IP theft incident to a wind turbine manufacturer or component supplier with LATAM market exposure — this would confirm the Sinovel pattern is active and current rather than historical precedent.
