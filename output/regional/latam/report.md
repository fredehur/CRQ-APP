## Why — Geopolitical Driver

Latin America's wind energy supply chain is under structural pressure from two converging forces that are reshaping the region's external dependency landscape. The U.S. Department of Commerce launched a Section 232 national security investigation into wind turbine and component imports in August 2025, corroborated by Reuters and KPMG Tax News Flash, citing foreign supply chain vulnerabilities and potential exposure of critical infrastructure to foreign state influence. If the investigation results in tariff recommendations — a foreseeable outcome given the investigation's explicit national security framing — import costs for wind turbine components sourced through PRC-linked supply chains would rise materially across all LATAM markets. Simultaneously, Chinese state-owned enterprises are advancing their footprint across Argentine, Brazilian, and Chilean energy sectors, corroborated by the Council on Foreign Relations and CSIS. These include CWE project awards in Chile's renewable sector and the Chalco-Rio Tinto acquisition of Brazilian aluminum assets — a critical input material for wind turbine components — deepening Beijing's structural presence across the material flows that underpin LATAM wind supply chains. Compounding both pressures, Argentina, Brazil, and Chile are simultaneously undergoing unprecedented regulatory changes in 2026: electricity market restructuring, battery storage sourcing reforms, and glacier and mining law revisions, documented by Wood Mackenzie and Americas Market Intelligence. The combined effect is a LATAM wind sector caught between U.S. trade security pressure on PRC-linked supply chains and the expanding commercial presence of the very state enterprises that supply them — within a regulatory environment too fluid to underwrite stable sourcing arrangements.

## How — Cyber Vector

Cyber collection for LATAM in this window produced zero grounded indicators. No named threat actor has been identified targeting LATAM wind energy operations, supply chain logistics platforms, or service management systems. No confirmed incident with a named target, method, or outcome was recovered involving turbine component suppliers, service scheduling infrastructure, or wind farm telemetry in the region. The cyber dimension of this brief therefore cannot be built on confirmed evidence — it rests entirely on analytical inference from the geopolitical signal set and acknowledged collection gaps.

Analyst judgment: the depth of PRC state-owned enterprise presence across LATAM wind component supply chains creates a structural pathway to supply chain integrity risk. Where a state-linked actor holds significant commercial access to the production or logistics of critical components, the possibility of unvalidated hardware or software dependencies entering the supply chain cannot be dismissed as a forward-looking concern. This remains an identified collection gap — not a confirmed threat vector. No intrusion activity, no adversary tradecraft, and no documented targeting of LATAM wind service infrastructure appears in the current collection window. Confidence on the cyber pillar is accordingly reduced and reflected in the B2 Admiralty rating rather than B1.

## So What — Business Impact

The supply chain disruption risk is most directly felt in AeroGrid's ability to fulfil its two active LATAM service contracts: the Enel Chile wind maintenance agreement and the Neoenergia turbine service contract in Brazil. Both depend on access to wind turbine components and replacement parts through supply chains now exposed to the compounding pressures documented above. If U.S. Section 232 tariffs are levied on PRC-linked wind components, the cost and availability of spare parts sourced through those channels will deteriorate — creating delivery risk for both contracts and operational risk for the 680 AeroGrid staff across the São Paulo and Santiago sites who depend on those supply lines to execute field service. AeroGrid holds no manufacturing presence in LATAM; the region's service-only model makes it a direct consumer of the component flows now under geopolitical pressure rather than a producer capable of buffering shortages through its own inventory.

The registered CRQ scenario most applicable to this risk environment is System intrusion (financial rank 3 globally by impact), read here as third-party and supply chain access risk enabled by the structural depth of PRC-linked vendor presence in the component supply chain. The LATAM scenario carries a VaCR of $0 — the CRQ register does not presently assign a quantified cyber exposure to LATAM operations, reflecting the absence of primary manufacturing assets in the region.

Watch for: U.S. Section 232 investigation issuing formal tariff recommendations specifically covering wind turbine components — this would convert the current trend into a confirmed sourcing cost event affecting both LATAM contracts. Watch for Chinese SOE project advances in Chile or Brazil entering wind maintenance or turbine service segments that directly compete with or complicate AeroGrid's supply line arrangements under the Enel and Neoenergia agreements. Watch for major regulatory reversals in Brazil's electricity market framework or Chile's wind energy investment rules that alter contract terms or component sourcing conditions for either active service agreement.
