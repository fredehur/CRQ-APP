# Speaking Notes — Quantitative Cyber Risk Model: Foundational Principles
### 45-minute government meeting

> **Presenter guidance:** Speak slowly and clearly at all times. Pause after each bullet point. Never introduce ideas that are not on the slide. Read the headline first, then walk through each bullet. Transitions are short — do not add new content between slides.

---

## Slide 1 — Opening
**Quantitative Cyber Risk Model — Foundational Principles**

---

Welcome everyone.

Today we are here to share the foundational principles behind our approach to quantifying cyber risk.

This session is about the *principles* — the shared understanding that sits underneath everything we do. How we think about cyber risk, and why we think about it this way.

We will go through ten principles, one at a time. Each one is short and clear.

Let us begin.

---

## Slide 2 — Principle 1
**Risk Is Continuous, Not Binary**

---

Read the title: *Risk Is Continuous, Not Binary.*

What does this mean?

Most people think of cyber risk as a simple question: are we safe, or have we been attacked?

We do not use that framing. Why not? Because it is not accurate.

[Bullet 1] Digital risk is not a switch that flips between "secure" and "compromised." It is a dynamic state. At any given moment, there is always some level of risk. It goes up, it goes down — but it never reaches zero, and it never becomes a single fixed number.

[Bullet 2] Cyber risk fluctuates over time. It changes when we make internal decisions. It changes when the outside world changes. It changes when adversaries change their behaviour.

[Bullet 3] This means security is not a project you complete and then move on from. It is ongoing risk management — the same way you manage financial risk or operational risk.

This is the first shift in how we think.

---

## Slide 3 — Principle 2
**Intentional Adversaries Drive Digital Risk**

---

Read the title: *Intentional Adversaries Drive Digital Risk.*

[Bullet 1] Behind most cyber incidents there is a person, or a group of people, making deliberate decisions. They have specific goals — financial gain, disruption, or access to information. This makes digital risk different from, say, a flood or an equipment failure.

[Bullet 2] This matters because defences work differently when you are dealing with an intelligent actor. A wall stops rain. But a determined person will look for a way around a wall. Our defences work by making the attack more costly and more difficult — not by making it impossible.

[Bullet 3] The goal, therefore, is to raise the effort required until the attack becomes unattractive. Until it is easier for the adversary to move on to a different target.

This principle shapes how we design our model. We are not modelling accidents. We are modelling decisions.

---

## Slide 4 — Principle 3
**Scenarios Are the Fundamental Unit of Risk**

---

Read the title: *Scenarios Are the Fundamental Unit of Risk.*

[Bullet 1] "Cyber risk" as a general concept is too vague to act on. We cannot manage something we cannot define. So we work with concrete scenarios. Specific types of incidents that have happened to organisations like ours and that could happen to us.

[Bullet 2] Each scenario is grounded in real-world evidence — what types of incidents occur, how often they occur, and what they cost. We do not invent these. They come from public threat intelligence and observed data across industries.

[Bullet 3] Using scenarios means everyone in the organisation — from the board to operational teams — is speaking about the same things, in the same terms. No ambiguity about what we mean when we say "cyber risk."

Scenarios are the shared language of the model.

---

## Slide 5 — Principle 4
**We Anchor Our Understanding in Peer Reality First**

---

Read the title: *We Anchor Our Understanding in Peer Reality First.*

[Bullet 1] Before we look at our own organisation, we start by looking outward. What is happening to organisations that are comparable to ours? What kinds of incidents do they experience?

[Bullet 2] We gather industry-wide probabilities — how likely different types of incidents are across comparable organisations. And we look at financial loss ranges — what those incidents typically cost when they occur.

[Bullet 3] This gives us a starting point that is grounded in publicly observable evidence. Not in assumptions. Not in internal opinions. We build from what we can observe in the world.

Starting with peers prevents us from either over- or underestimating our own risk.

---

## Slide 6 — Principle 5
**Our Own Environment Shapes Likelihood**

---

Read the title: *Our Own Environment Shapes Likelihood.*

[Bullet 1] Once we have the peer baseline, we interpret it through the lens of our own organisation. We ask: given who we are and how we operate, how does that change the picture?

[Bullet 2] We look at our architecture. Our processes. Our governance. Our technology. Our organisational choices. All of these affect how exposed we are and how likely an incident is to succeed.

[Bullet 3] Every security measure we have in place makes it harder for an incident to succeed. It changes the likelihood. This is not guesswork — it is interpretation based on evidence.

The peer baseline tells us where to start. Our own environment tells us how to adjust.

---

## Slide 7 — Principle 6
**Impact Is Expressed in Financial Terms**

---

Read the title: *Impact Is Expressed in Financial Terms.*

[Bullet 1] Cyber risk only becomes truly meaningful when it is expressed in the language of the organisation: money. We translate cyber risk into direct financial impact — specifically, the effect on the organisation's financial performance.

[Bullet 2] We use direct, measurable financial consequences. Not subjective ratings. Not colour codes. Not "high, medium, low." Numbers.

[Bullet 3] This makes cyber risk objective, auditable, and directly comparable to every other type of risk the organisation manages. It sits on the same scale as operational risk, financial risk, and commercial risk.

Financial terms create a common language across all functions.

---

## Slide 8 — Principle 7
**Risk Is Communicated as Distributions, Not Singular Values**

---

Read the title: *Risk Is Communicated as Distributions, Not Singular Values.*

[Bullet 1] No single number can tell the full story of cyber risk. If we give you one number, we are hiding information. The reality is that there is a range of possible outcomes — and we communicate that range.

[Bullet 2] We use a set of complementary metrics. What do we expect to lose on average over time? What does a bad year look like? What does a very rare but severe event look like? What is the full shape of uncertainty across all possible loss levels? Each of these answers a different question.

[Bullet 3] Different people in the organisation need different views. A board member and an operations manager ask different questions. Our metrics are designed to answer all of them.

Risk is not one number. It is a shape.

---

## Slide 9 — Principle 8
**Risk Communication Must Be Standardised and Actionable**

---

Read the title: *Risk Communication Must Be Standardised and Actionable.*

To avoid ambiguity, every significant risk statement we produce follows the same four-part structure:

[Point 1] First — the scenario name. What type of incident are we talking about?

[Point 2] Second — the organisation-specific likelihood. How probable is this for us?

[Point 3] Third — the financial impact view. What would it cost if it occurred?

[Point 4] Fourth — the key levers that would materially reduce that risk. What can we do about it?

Every risk statement includes all four. Always. This ensures that anyone reading our output — regardless of their background — receives the same information, structured the same way.

---

## Slide 10 — Principle 9
**Transparency and Traceability Are Non-Negotiable**

---

Read the title: *Transparency and Traceability Are Non-Negotiable.*

[Bullet 1] Every output we produce must be explainable, traceable, and supported by documented assumptions. Nothing comes from a black box.

[Bullet 2] Every figure is linked back to its source — the peer data it came from, the scenario it relates to, the organisational context that shaped it.

[Bullet 3] Any stakeholder — an auditor, an executive, a regulator — should be able to take any financial figure we produce and trace it back to its origin, step by step.

Trust in the model depends entirely on this principle.

---

## Slide 11 — Principle 10
**The Framework Is Living and Must Adapt**

---

Read the title: *The Framework Is Living and Must Adapt.*

[Bullet 1] The digital landscape changes rapidly. New threats emerge. Old ones evolve. Our model must keep up.

[Bullet 2] We maintain a regular governance cadence — scheduled reviews where we refresh the scenario definitions, the peer benchmark data, and our understanding of the organisation's own environment.

[Bullet 3] This model is never finished. It is continuously maintained. Digital risk quantification is a practice, not a product.

A model that does not adapt quickly becomes inaccurate. This principle protects against that.

---

## Slide 12 — How These Principles Are Used
**Three purposes**

---

Read the title: *How These Principles Are Used.*

These ten principles serve three purposes in everything we do:

[Alignment] First — alignment. Every stakeholder, from technical experts to board members, must understand the philosophical basis of the model before engaging with any technical detail. These principles create that shared foundation.

[Interpretation] Second — interpretation. These principles guide how outputs should be read and applied. When you receive a risk figure, these principles tell you what it means and what it does not mean.

[Governance] Third — governance. These principles define the standards we hold ourselves to: transparency, regular refresh, and consistent communication across the organisation.
