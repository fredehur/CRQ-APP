§ 5 · Board-action scenario Standalone · evidence-anchored
S-07MED · Morocco

Sustained civil unrest in the Casablanca corridor near Cape Wind.

Political-security·Crown-jewel exposure·Horizon: 0–12 weeks
HIGH · MED likelihood
SeverityHIGH LikelihoodMED AdmiraltyB2 Δ vs Q1↑ from MED

The April general election closed without a clear coalition. Youth unemployment and regional economic stress remain the structural drivers; the post-election window is the acute one.

Protest activity clustered in urban centres through the polling period and has not fully subsided. Security-force posture in the Casablanca corridor is elevated and is expected to remain so until a governing coalition is named. The Cape Wind site itself has not been the object of protest; the proximate risks are access-road disruption, expat movement, and secondary effects on local vendor continuity.

In parallel, APT28 scanning against Moroccan energy ASNs has run coincident with the election calendar. The SOC has observed no anomalies at Cape Wind, but the cyber-physical coupling means the scenario is being assessed as a joint signal: state-actor cyber posture is mirroring state-actor physical interest, and the monitoring posture reflects that.

Base case is normalisation over a 6–12 week horizon as coalition formation completes. Tail risk is prolonged coalition friction combined with economic stress — that case reaches the October board.

Organisational implications
  • Offtake continuity — local grid interface
  • Expat safety & movement (12 FTE on site)
  • Local reputation with host ministry
  • Insurance premium renewal (Q4 cycle)
Baselines moved
  • Regional posture: MED → HIGH
  • Site watch: normal → raised
  • Expat travel: permitted → essential only
Drivers
  1. Post-election coalition friction; no clear plurality winner.
  2. Youth unemployment at structural highs; regional wage stagnation.
  3. Broader regional economic stress (currency, remittances).
  4. Historic pattern of post-poll protest activity in urban centres.
Actions & governance asks
  1. Continue raised site watch at Cape Wind through end Q3.
  2. Reassess severity at the October board based on coalition outcome.
  3. Maintain SOC joint-signal monitoring on Moroccan energy ASNs.
  4. No board approval needed this quarter; noting for awareness.

§ 6 · Board-action scenario Standalone · evidence-anchored
S-11NCE · EU wind operators

Cyber intrusion into AeroGrid OT via a shared SCADA vendor.

Cyber · campaign-driven·Shared-vendor exposure·Horizon: 0–8 weeks
HIGH · MED-HIGH likelihood
SeverityHIGH LikelihoodMED-HIGH AdmiraltyB1 Δ vs Q1↑ new evidence

The SolarGlare ransomware campaign is now confirmed at two German wind operators. Four additional EU operators report probable targeting. The campaign's TTPs match a single actor set, and the shared-vendor vector is active.

AeroGrid NCE sites run the same Vendor X SCADA stack implicated in the confirmed intrusions. Patch availability for CVE-2026-1847 is current; portfolio deployment stands at 40% as of the issue date. CVE-2026-1722 (grid HMI) has a workaround only, with two sites affected. Vendor X's access into AeroGrid environments has not been reviewed since Q3 2025.

The governance question is no longer whether NCE is exposed — it is whether the current patch cadence and vendor-access posture are sufficient given the campaign's demonstrated velocity. SOC monitoring has been raised; no intrusions observed to date.

If the campaign reaches an AeroGrid operator, regulatory reporting is triggered under the pending EU OT/ICS framework and under existing national obligations. That is the downside tail case.

Organisational implications
  • Generation availability across NCE fleet
  • OT data integrity (metering, control)
  • Regulatory reporting (national + draft EU)
  • Insurance cyber-rider — review trigger
Baselines moved
  • NCE cyber: held → pressured
  • Vendor X risk: MED → HIGH
  • Patch SLA: 30d → 14d for 1847
Drivers
  1. SolarGlare campaign confirmed at 2 DE operators; 4 probable.
  2. Shared SCADA vendor (Vendor X v4.2) across the NCE fleet.
  3. CVE-2026-1847 patch at 40% rollout — residual exposure.
  4. Third-party vendor access unreviewed since Q3 2025.
Actions & governance asks
  1. Board ask: endorse accelerated CVE-2026-1847 patching — 14-day SLA.
  2. Board ask: approve a third-party vendor access review, scope Vendor X + two adjacent.
  3. SOC maintains raised monitoring posture until campaign subsides.
  4. Report back at October board with patch completion and review findings.

§ 7 · Watched scenario · supply-chain Standalone · evidence-anchored
S-14APAC · blade supply

Supply-chain disruption on turbine blades via a single-source supplier.

Supply chain·Project-delivery risk·Horizon: 2027 delivery window
MEDIUM · LOW-MED likelihood
SeverityMED LikelihoodLOW-MED AdmiraltyB2 Δ vs Q1↓ transit eased

Blade supply for the 2027 project cohort currently relies on a single Chinese supplier. Transit conditions in the APAC corridor improved this quarter, which eases the short-term logistics risk but leaves the structural single-source exposure intact.

The governance concern is strategic, not tactical. Geopolitical export-restriction risk — against the backdrop of ongoing US-China trade policy shifts — is the case that would bind. A disruption at the supplier in late 2026 would propagate through the 2027 delivery timeline and create an observable EBITDA impact in 2027–28.

Procurement has a qualified second-source programme in early stages. Target qualification is Q4 2026; that date is the board-level milestone to track. No quarterly board action is requested; inclusion here is for visibility and milestone anchoring.

Organisational implications
  • 2027 project delivery timeline
  • EBITDA exposure 2027–28
  • Procurement programme cost
  • Customer commitments (2 offtake contracts)
Baselines moved
  • APAC transit: watched → stable
  • Supplier concentration: held
  • 2nd-source: on plan (Q4 2026)
Drivers
  1. Single-source Chinese supplier for 2027 blade cohort.
  2. Geopolitical export-restriction risk — trade policy shifts.
  3. Transit corridor improved (short-term), structural exposure held.
  4. Second-source qualification in early stages.
Actions & governance asks
  1. Procurement to complete second-source qualification by Q4 2026.
  2. Treasury to model 2027–28 EBITDA under disruption case.
  3. Reconfirm at October board; escalate to board-action if 2nd-source slips past Q1 2027.
  4. No board approval required this quarter.

§ 8 · Methodology Sources · ratings · reading guide

How to read this brief.

One page. No illustrations. A board member should be able to tell, at a glance, what has changed since last quarter and what a change in rating means.

Sources

This brief draws on a combination of commercial intelligence feeds, open-source discovery, and internal Regional Security Manager (RSM) reporting. Every assertion on pages 2–7 traces to a numbered evidence anchor appended to the scenario it supports.

Commercial
Seerist (Control Risks) — geopolitical feed, confidence-rated.
OSINT
Firecrawl + Tavily discovery across accredited regional media.
Cyber
CERT-EU, CISA, NCSC, vendor advisories.
Internal
RSM weekly INTSUMs (MED, NCE, APAC, LATAM, AME).
Rating system

Severity and confidence are rated independently. Severity captures the potential consequence to AeroGrid; likelihood captures the probability within the stated horizon; Admiralty captures source and information grading.

Severity
CRITICAL · HIGH · MEDIUM · MONITOR.
Likelihood
HIGH · MED-HIGH · MEDIUM · LOW-MED · LOW.
Admiralty
A1 (near-certain) — F6 (unverifiable). Typical range: B1–C2.
Confidence
NATO framework: HIGH / MODERATE / LOW.
What the board should read into
B2 → A1
A scenario moving from B2 to A1 on Admiralty should be treated as near-certain. Governance response is warranted even if severity is unchanged.
MED → HIGH
A severity change from MEDIUM to HIGH warrants a board conversation in the next cycle — not necessarily an action, but not silent either.
Quiet region
A region in baseline with no new triggers is a signal in itself. The absence of escalation is an intelligence product — not an omission.
Joint signal
Cyber and physical activity in the same country should be read as one signal, not two. S-07 (MED) is the current example.
Evidence chain
Every assertion traces to an E- or C-prefixed anchor. If an anchor is missing, challenge the assertion.
